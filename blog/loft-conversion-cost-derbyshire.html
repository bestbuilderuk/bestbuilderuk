Key Factors That Influence the Cost

1. Type of Conversion

As shown above, the structural complexity of the conversion is the biggest cost driver. A simple Velux conversion that doesn't alter the roofline will always be cheaper than a dormer or mansard that requires significant structural work.

2. Size of the Loft

A larger floor area will naturally require more materials and labour. The usable area of your loft depends on the pitch of your roof and the height at the ridge.

3. Roof Structure

Older homes with traditional 'cut rafter' roofs are often easier and cheaper to convert than modern homes with 'trussed' roofs, which require more structural reinforcement. A structural engineer's assessment is always recommended.

4. Specification and Finish

The final cost is heavily influenced by your choice of finishes:

  • Bathrooms: A simple WC will be much cheaper than a luxury en-suite with a walk-in shower and underfloor heating.
  • Windows: Standard Velux windows are cheaper than a large dormer or a Juliet balcony.
  • Staircase: A custom-made oak staircase will cost more than a standard softwood design.
  • Fixtures and Fittings: High-end lighting, flooring, and built-in storage will increase the budget.

5. Planning Permission and Building Regulations

While many loft conversions fall under Permitted Development, complex projects may require planning permission, which adds fees and time. All conversions require Building Regulations approval, which involves fees for inspections.

Is a Loft Conversion a Good Investment?

Absolutely. In most cases, the value added to your home will exceed the cost of the project. According to industry data, a well-executed loft conversion can add between 15% and 20% to a property's value. It's also a more cost-effective way to gain an extra room compared to moving house, once you factor in stamp duty, legal fees, and removal costs.
